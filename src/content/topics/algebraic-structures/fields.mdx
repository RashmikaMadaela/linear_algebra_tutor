## Motivation

A **field** captures the essential algebraic structure of the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$, real numbers $\mathbb{R}$, and complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ — sets where we can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (by non-zero elements) freely. Fields are the scalars over which vector spaces are defined.

## Definition of a Field

<DefinitionBox number={6} title="Field">
A **field** is a non-empty set $\mathbb{F}$ together with two binary operations, **addition** $(+)$ and **multiplication** $(\cdot)$, satisfying the following axioms:

**Addition axioms** — $(\mathbb{F}, +)$ is an abelian group:
1. **Closure under $+$**: $a + b \in \mathbb{F}$
2. **Associativity**: $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$
3. **Additive identity**: $\exists\, 0 \in \mathbb{F}$ such that $a + 0 = a$
4. **Additive inverses**: $\exists\, (-a) \in \mathbb{F}$ such that $a + (-a) = 0$
5. **Commutativity**: $a + b = b + a$

**Multiplication axioms** — $(\mathbb{F} \setminus \{0\}, \cdot)$ is an abelian group:
6. **Closure under $\cdot$**: $a \cdot b \in \mathbb{F}$
7. **Associativity**: $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$
8. **Multiplicative identity**: $\exists\, 1 \in \mathbb{F}$, $1 \neq 0$, such that $a \cdot 1 = a$
9. **Multiplicative inverses**: for $a \neq 0$, $\exists\, a^{-1} \in \mathbb{F}$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = 1$
10. **Commutativity**: $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$

**Distributivity** — linking $+$ and $\cdot$:
11. $a \cdot (b + c) = a \cdot b + a \cdot c$
</DefinitionBox>

## Standard Examples

The following are the fields most commonly used in linear algebra:

| Field | Set | Notes |
|-------|-----|-------|
| $\mathbb{Q}$ | Rational numbers | Smallest field of characteristic 0 |
| $\mathbb{R}$ | Real numbers | Complete ordered field |
| $\mathbb{C}$ | Complex numbers | Algebraically closed |
| $\mathbb{F}_p$ | $\{0, 1, \ldots, p-1\}$, $p$ prime | Finite field of order $p$ |

## Non-Examples

Not every set with two operations is a field. Here are important cases to recognize:

- $(\mathbb{Z}, +, \cdot)$: **Not a field** — $2$ has no multiplicative inverse in $\mathbb{Z}$ (since $\frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{Z}$).
- $(\mathbb{Z}_4, +_4, \cdot_4)$: **Not a field** — $2 \in \mathbb{Z}_4$ has no inverse: $2 \cdot k \in \{0, 2\}$ for all $k \in \mathbb{Z}_4$.
- $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is a field **if and only if** $n$ is prime.

## Key Theorems

<TheoremBox number={5} title="Characteristic of a Field">
For any field $\mathbb{F}$, either:
- $n \cdot 1 \neq 0$ for all positive integers $n$ (we say $\text{char}(\mathbb{F}) = 0$), or
- there exists a **smallest** positive integer $p$ such that $p \cdot 1 = 0$ (we say $\text{char}(\mathbb{F}) = p$), and $p$ must be **prime**.

Examples: $\text{char}(\mathbb{Q}) = \text{char}(\mathbb{R}) = \text{char}(\mathbb{C}) = 0$; $\text{char}(\mathbb{F}_p) = p$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={6} title="Zero Product Property" proofSketch={<>If <M math="ab = 0" /> and <M math="a \neq 0" />, multiply both sides on the left by <M math="a^{-1}" />: <M math="a^{-1}(ab) = a^{-1} \cdot 0" />, giving <M math="b = 0" />.</>}>
In a field $\mathbb{F}$, if $a \cdot b = 0$ then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={7} title="Basic Consequences of Field Axioms" proofSketch={<>For (i): <M math="a \cdot 0 = a \cdot (0 + 0) = a \cdot 0 + a \cdot 0" />; subtract <M math="a \cdot 0" /> from both sides. For (ii): <M math="(-a)b = ((-1)a)b = (-1)(ab) = -(ab)" />.</>}>
For all $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. $a \cdot 0 = 0$
2. $(-a) \cdot b = -(a \cdot b) = a \cdot (-b)$
3. $(-a)(-b) = ab$
</TheoremBox>

## Why Fields Matter for Linear Algebra

Vector spaces are defined over a field $\mathbb{F}$. The field provides the **scalars** used to scale vectors. The axioms of a field are precisely what is needed to make the theory of vector spaces (including basis, dimension, eigenvalues, etc.) work cleanly.

In MA2034, we typically work over $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, but all results hold over any field unless otherwise stated.

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Fields">
- A **field** $(\mathbb{F}, +, \cdot)$ has two abelian group structures linked by distributivity.
- Standard fields: $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, and $\mathbb{F}_p$ for prime $p$.
- $\mathbb{Z}$ is **not** a field (no multiplicative inverses for $n \neq \pm 1$).
- $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is a field $\iff$ $n$ is prime.
- The **characteristic** of a field is either $0$ or a prime $p$.
- Fields are the scalar systems over which vector spaces are defined — mastering fields is essential for all that follows.
</SummaryBox>
