## The Goal of Diagonalization

Working with diagonal matrices is easy: powers, exponentials, and inverses are all computed entry-by-entry. Given a matrix <M math="A" />, we seek an invertible <M math="P" /> such that <M math="P^{-1}AP = D" /> is diagonal. When this is possible, we say <M math="A" /> is **diagonalizable**.

---

## Definition and Criteria

<DefinitionBox number={28} title="Diagonalizable Matrix">
A matrix <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" /> is **diagonalizable** over <M math="\mathbb{F}" /> if it is similar to a diagonal matrix, i.e., there exists invertible <M math="P" /> and diagonal <M math="D" /> such that

<BM math="A = PDP^{-1}, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad P^{-1}AP = D." />

The columns of <M math="P" /> are eigenvectors of <M math="A" />, and the diagonal entries of <M math="D" /> are the corresponding eigenvalues.
</DefinitionBox>

<TheoremBox
  number={33}
  title="Diagonalization Criterion"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      <M math="(\Rightarrow)" /> If <M math="P^{-1}AP = D = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)" />, then <M math="AP = PD" />. Writing <M math="P = [\mathbf{p}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{p}_n]" />: column <M math="j" /> of <M math="AP" /> is <M math="A\mathbf{p}_j" />, column <M math="j" /> of <M math="PD" /> is <M math="\lambda_j \mathbf{p}_j" />. So <M math="A\mathbf{p}_j = \lambda_j \mathbf{p}_j" /> for all <M math="j" />. Since <M math="P" /> is invertible its columns are nonzero, so they are eigenvectors.
      <br />
      <M math="(\Leftarrow)" /> Conversely, if <M math="A" /> has <M math="n" /> LI eigenvectors <M math="\mathbf{p}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{p}_n" /> with eigenvalues <M math="\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n" />, set <M math="P = [\mathbf{p}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{p}_n]" /> (invertible by LI). Then <M math="AP = PD" />, so <M math="P^{-1}AP = D" />. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  The following are equivalent for <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" />:

  1. <M math="A" /> is diagonalizable over <M math="\mathbb{F}" />.
  2. <M math="A" /> has <M math="n" /> linearly independent eigenvectors in <M math="\mathbb{F}^n" />.
  3. <M math="\mathbb{F}^n" /> has a basis of eigenvectors of <M math="A" />.
  4. For every eigenvalue <M math="\lambda_i" />, the geometric multiplicity equals the algebraic multiplicity: <M math="m_g(\lambda_i) = m_a(\lambda_i)" />.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox
  number={34}
  title="Distinct Eigenvalues Imply Diagonalizable"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      If <M math="A" /> has <M math="n" /> distinct eigenvalues <M math="\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n" />, then by Theorem 28 (LI eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues), any choice of one eigenvector per eigenvalue gives <M math="n" /> LI eigenvectors. Apply Theorem 33. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  If <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" /> has <M math="n" /> **distinct** eigenvalues in <M math="\mathbb{F}" />, then <M math="A" /> is diagonalizable over <M math="\mathbb{F}" />.

  **Note:** This is a **sufficient** but not **necessary** condition — matrices with repeated eigenvalues can still be diagonalizable (e.g., <M math="A = 2I" />).
</TheoremBox>

## The Diagonalization Algorithm

Given <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" />:

1. **Find eigenvalues**: Solve <M math="p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = 0" />.
2. **Check**: All roots must lie in <M math="\mathbb{F}" /> (for real diagonalization, no complex roots).
3. **Find eigenspaces**: For each <M math="\lambda_i" />, find a basis for <M math="\ker(A - \lambda_i I)" />.
4. **Check multiplicities**: Verify <M math="m_g(\lambda_i) = m_a(\lambda_i)" /> for each <M math="\lambda_i" />.
5. **Build** <M math="P" />: Concatenate the eigenvector bases as columns.
6. **Write** <M math="D" />: Diagonal entries = corresponding eigenvalues.

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways">
- <M math="A" /> is **diagonalizable** iff it has <M math="n" /> LI eigenvectors iff <M math="m_g(\lambda_i) = m_a(\lambda_i)" /> for all eigenvalues.
- <strong>Algorithm:</strong> Find eigenvalues → find eigenspaces → build <M math="P" /> from eigenvectors, <M math="D" /> from eigenvalues.
- <strong>Sufficient condition:</strong> <M math="n" /> distinct eigenvalues ⟹ diagonalizable.
- **Power formula:** <M math="A^k = PD^kP^{-1}" /> — diagonalization makes powers trivial.
- Not all matrices are diagonalizable (even over <M math="\mathbb{C}" />) — defective matrices require Jordan form.
- Diagonalizability depends on the field: a matrix may be diagonalizable over <M math="\mathbb{C}" /> but not over <M math="\mathbb{R}" />.
</SummaryBox>
