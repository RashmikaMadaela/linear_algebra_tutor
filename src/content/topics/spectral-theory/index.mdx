## Section Overview

An eigenvector of a linear operator $T$ is a vector that $T$ merely *scales* rather than rotates or shears.
The corresponding scalar is the **eigenvalue**.
These special vectors and values unlock the deepest structure of a linear operator — telling us whether it can be diagonalized, what its long-run behavior is, and how it decomposes.

Spectral theory is the heart of applied linear algebra: it underlies Google's PageRank, quantum mechanics (observables), principal component analysis, and stability analysis of dynamical systems.

### The Five Key Ideas

1. **Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors** — definition, geometric picture, first examples
2. **Characteristic Polynomial** — $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ produces the spectrum
3. **Similar Matrices** — same linear map, different bases; which properties are invariant?
4. **Algebraic & Geometric Multiplicities** — when does an eigenvalue "fill" the space?
5. **Diagonalization** — conditions, algorithm, and the diagonal form $A = PDP^{-1}$

### Definitions in this section

- **Def 27** — Algebraic multiplicity $m_a(\lambda)$ and geometric multiplicity $m_g(\lambda)$
- **Def 28** — Diagonalizable matrix/operator

### Theorems in this section
- **Thm 28** — Eigenspaces for distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent
- **Thm 29** — Similar matrices share characteristic polynomial, eigenvalues, trace, determinant
- **Thm 30** — $1 \leq m_g(\lambda) \leq m_a(\lambda)$
- **Thm 31** — $A$ is diagonalizable $\iff$ geometric multiplicity = algebraic multiplicity for all $\lambda$
- **Thm 32** — Cayley–Hamilton theorem: $p_A(A) = 0$
- **Thm 33** — Symmetric real matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors
