## The Characteristic Equation

Finding eigenvalues reduces to finding which scalars <M math="\lambda" /> make <M math="A - \lambda I" /> **singular** (non-invertible). A square matrix is singular iff its determinant is zero, giving us the characteristic equation.

---

## Definitions and Theorems

<DefinitionBox number={24} title="Characteristic Polynomial">
Let <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" />. The **characteristic polynomial** of <M math="A" /> is

<BM math="p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A)." />

This is a polynomial of degree <M math="n" /> in <M math="\lambda" /> with leading coefficient <M math="1" /> (monic). Some texts use <M math="\det(A - \lambda I) = (-1)^n p_A(\lambda)" /> — both conventions yield the same roots.

The **characteristic equation** is <M math="p_A(\lambda) = 0" />.
</DefinitionBox>

<TheoremBox
  number={29}
  title="Eigenvalues are Roots of the Characteristic Polynomial"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      <M math="\lambda" /> is an eigenvalue of <M math="A" /> iff <M math="A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}" /> for some <M math="\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}" />, iff <M math="(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}" /> has a non-trivial solution, iff <M math="A - \lambda I" /> is singular, iff <M math="\det(\lambda I - A) = 0" />, iff <M math="p_A(\lambda) = 0" />. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  A scalar <M math="\lambda \in \mathbb{F}" /> is an eigenvalue of <M math="A" /> if and only if

  <BM math="p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = 0." />
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox
  number={30}
  title="Structure of the Characteristic Polynomial"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      Expand <M math="\det(\lambda I - A)" />. The term <M math="\lambda^n" /> comes only from the main diagonal product. The coefficient of <M math="\lambda^{n-1}" /> comes from choosing <M math="n-1" /> diagonal terms <M math="(\lambda - a_{ii})" />, which gives <M math="-\operatorname{tr}(A)" />. Setting <M math="\lambda = 0" />: <M math="p_A(0) = \det(-A) = (-1)^n\det(A)" />. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  For an <M math="n\times n" /> matrix <M math="A" />, the characteristic polynomial has the form

  <BM math="p_A(\lambda) = \lambda^n - \operatorname{tr}(A)\,\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + (-1)^n\det(A)." />

  In particular:
  - **Sum of eigenvalues** (counted with multiplicity) = <M math="\operatorname{tr}(A)" />
  - **Product of eigenvalues** (counted with multiplicity) = <M math="\det(A)" />
  - By the **Fundamental Theorem of Algebra**, over <M math="\mathbb{C}" /> every <M math="n\times n" /> matrix has exactly <M math="n" /> eigenvalues (counting multiplicity).
</TheoremBox>

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways">
- Find eigenvalues by solving <M math="\det(\lambda I - A) = 0" /> — the **characteristic equation**.
- The characteristic polynomial <M math="p_A(\lambda)" /> has degree <M math="n" />, with <M math="\operatorname{tr}(A)" /> and <M math="\det(A)" /> as coefficients.
- Eigenvalues of **triangular matrices** are the diagonal entries.
- Over <M math="\mathbb{C}" />, every matrix has <M math="n" /> eigenvalues (with multiplicity).
- Once eigenvalues are found, get eigenvectors by solving <M math="(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}" />.
- The **sum** of eigenvalues = <M math="\operatorname{tr}(A)" />; the **product** = <M math="\det(A)" />.
</SummaryBox>
