## Two Notions of Multiplicity

A repeated eigenvalue has two different "counts":
- **Algebraic multiplicity** — how many times it appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial.
- **Geometric multiplicity** — the dimension of its eigenspace (how many independent eigenvectors it has).

These need not be equal, and their relationship governs diagonalizability.

---

## Definitions

<DefinitionBox number={26} title="Algebraic Multiplicity">
Let <M math="\lambda_0" /> be an eigenvalue of <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" />. The **algebraic multiplicity** of <M math="\lambda_0" /> is the multiplicity of <M math="\lambda_0" /> as a root of the characteristic polynomial:

<BM math="p_A(\lambda) = (\lambda - \lambda_0)^{m_a} \cdot q(\lambda), \quad q(\lambda_0) \neq 0." />

We denote the algebraic multiplicity by <M math="m_a(\lambda_0)" />.
</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox number={27} title="Geometric Multiplicity">
The **geometric multiplicity** of eigenvalue <M math="\lambda_0" /> is the dimension of the corresponding eigenspace:

<BM math="m_g(\lambda_0) = \dim E_{\lambda_0} = \dim \ker(A - \lambda_0 I) = n - \operatorname{rank}(A - \lambda_0 I)." />
</DefinitionBox>

## The Key Inequality

<TheoremBox
  number={32}
  title="Multiplicity Inequality"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      <strong>Lower bound <M math="m_g \geq 1" />:</strong> Since <M math="\lambda_0" /> is an eigenvalue, <M math="\ker(A-\lambda_0 I) \neq \{\mathbf{0}\}" />, so <M math="m_g \geq 1" />.
      <br />
      <strong>Upper bound <M math="m_g \leq m_a" />:</strong> Let <M math="k = m_g" /> and choose a basis <M math="\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k" /> for <M math="E_{\lambda_0}" />, extended to a basis <M math="\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n" /> of <M math="\mathbb{F}^n" />. In this basis, <M math="A" /> has the block form <M math="\begin{pmatrix}\lambda_0 I_k & * \\ 0 & B\end{pmatrix}" />, so <M math="p_A(\lambda) = (\lambda-\lambda_0)^k \cdot p_B(\lambda)" />, meaning <M math="(\lambda-\lambda_0)^k \mid p_A(\lambda)" />. Hence <M math="k \leq m_a" />. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  For any eigenvalue <M math="\lambda_0" /> of <M math="A" />:

  <BM math="1 \leq m_g(\lambda_0) \leq m_a(\lambda_0)." />

  A matrix is **diagonalizable** if and only if <M math="m_g(\lambda_i) = m_a(\lambda_i)" /> for every eigenvalue <M math="\lambda_i" />.
</TheoremBox>

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways">
- **Algebraic multiplicity** <M math="m_a(\lambda_0)" />: multiplicity as root of <M math="p_A(\lambda)" />.
- **Geometric multiplicity** <M math="m_g(\lambda_0)" />: dimension of eigenspace <M math="\ker(A - \lambda_0 I)" />.
- Always: <M math="1 \leq m_g(\lambda_0) \leq m_a(\lambda_0)" />.
- **Diagonalizable** iff <M math="m_g = m_a" /> for every eigenvalue.
- A defect <M math="m_g < m_a" /> indicates a **Jordan block** structure — the matrix cannot be diagonalized.
- For simple eigenvalues (<M math="m_a = 1" />): always <M math="m_g = 1" />, never a problem.
</SummaryBox>
