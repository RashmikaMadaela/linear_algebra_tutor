## Motivation

When a linear map <M math="T: V \to V" /> acts on a vector, it usually rotates *and* stretches it. But for certain special vectors <M math="\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}" />, the map only *scales* — the direction is preserved and only the magnitude changes:

<BM math="T(\mathbf{v}) = \lambda \mathbf{v}." />

These vectors reveal the intrinsic "axes" of a linear map and lead to the most powerful computational tool in linear algebra: **diagonalization**.

---

## Definitions

<DefinitionBox number={22} title="Eigenvalue and Eigenvector">
Let <M math="T: V \to V" /> be a linear operator on a vector space <M math="V" /> over a field <M math="\mathbb{F}" />. A scalar <M math="\lambda \in \mathbb{F}" /> is an **eigenvalue** of <M math="T" /> if there exists a nonzero vector <M math="\mathbf{v} \in V" /> such that

<BM math="T(\mathbf{v}) = \lambda \mathbf{v}." />

Such a vector <M math="\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}" /> is called an **eigenvector** of <M math="T" /> corresponding to <M math="\lambda" />.

**For matrices:** <M math="\lambda" /> is an eigenvalue of <M math="A \in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{F})" /> if <M math="A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}" /> for some <M math="\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}" />.
</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox number={23} title="Eigenspace and Spectrum">
The **eigenspace** of <M math="T" /> corresponding to eigenvalue <M math="\lambda" /> is

<BM math="E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I) = \{\mathbf{v} \in V : T(\mathbf{v}) = \lambda \mathbf{v}\}." />

This is a subspace of <M math="V" /> (it includes <M math="\mathbf{0}" /> plus all eigenvectors for <M math="\lambda" />).

The **spectrum** of <M math="T" /> is the set of all eigenvalues: <M math="\sigma(T) = \{\lambda \in \mathbb{F} : E_\lambda \neq \{\mathbf{0}\}\}" />.
</DefinitionBox>

## Key Theorem

<TheoremBox
  number={28}
  title="LI Eigenvectors for Distinct Eigenvalues"
  proofSketch={
    <>
      Induct on <M math="k" />. Base case <M math="k=1" />: a single eigenvector is nonzero, hence LI.
      Inductive step: suppose <M math="\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_{k-1}" /> are LI and assume
      <M math="\sum_{i=1}^k \alpha_i \mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}" />.
      Apply <M math="T" /> to get <M math="\sum \alpha_i \lambda_i \mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}" />. Subtract <M math="\lambda_k" /> times the original: <M math="\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \alpha_i(\lambda_i - \lambda_k)\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}" />. By induction all <M math="\alpha_i = 0" /> for <M math="i < k" /> (since <M math="\lambda_i \neq \lambda_k" />), then <M math="\alpha_k = 0" /> too. ∎
    </>
  }
>
  If <M math="\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_k" /> are **distinct** eigenvalues of <M math="T" />, and <M math="\mathbf{v}_i \in E_{\lambda_i}" /> are corresponding nonzero eigenvectors, then <M math="\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}" /> is **linearly independent**.

  **Corollary:** An <M math="n \times n" /> matrix with <M math="n" /> distinct eigenvalues is diagonalizable.
</TheoremBox>

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways">
- An eigenvector is a nonzero vector whose direction is preserved (only scaled) by <M math="T" />.
- The eigenspace <M math="E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I)" /> is a subspace; its nonzero elements are the eigenvectors.
- To find eigenvalues: solve <M math="\det(A - \lambda I) = 0" /> (next lesson).
- To find eigenvectors: for each <M math="\lambda" />, solve <M math="(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}" />.
- Eigenvectors from **distinct** eigenvalues are always linearly independent.
- Geometrically: eigenvalues measure scaling along invariant directions.
</SummaryBox>
