## Big Idea

With an inner product defined, two fundamental inequalities follow immediately.
The **Cauchy-Schwarz inequality** says that $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$ — the inner product is bounded by the product of the lengths.
This single inequality underlies the definition of angle, the concept of orthogonality, and virtually every approximation theorem in the course.
The **triangle inequality** extends from geometry: the straight-line distance is never longer than going via a detour.

## Intuition

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the dot product formula gives $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$.
Since $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$, we always have $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$.
Cauchy-Schwarz says this holds in **any** inner product space — including function spaces and matrix spaces.

The triangle inequality $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$ is the familiar statement that the length of a side of a triangle is at most the sum of the other two sides.

## Theorems

<TheoremBox number={18} title="Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality" proofSketch={<>For any $t\in\mathbb{R}$, expand $0\leq\|\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \langle\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v}\rangle$ into a quadratic in $t$. The discriminant of this non-negative quadratic must be $\leq 0$, which gives exactly $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$.</>}>

Let $V$ be an inner product space. For all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v} \in V$:

<BM math="|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|" />

**Equality** holds if and only if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent (one is a scalar multiple of the other).
</TheoremBox>

**Full proof (Thm 18):**

If $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ both sides are $0$. Assume $\mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0}$. For any scalar $t$:
<BM math="0 \leq \|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \langle\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v}\rangle = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 - t\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle - \bar{t}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + |t|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2" />

Set $t = \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle / \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$. Then $\bar{t} = \overline{\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle}/\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$ and after substitution:
<BM math="0 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 - \frac{|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|^2}{\|\mathbf{v}\|^2}" />

Rearranging: $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$. Taking square roots gives the result. $\blacksquare$

<TheoremBox number={19} title="Triangle Inequality" proofSketch={<>Expand $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$, then apply Cauchy-Schwarz to bound $\operatorname{Re}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle \leq |\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$.</>}>

For all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v} \in V$:
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|" />
</TheoremBox>

**Full proof (Thm 19):**
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\rangle = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + \overline{\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle} + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2" />

Since $\operatorname{Re}(z) \leq |z|$ for any $z\in\mathbb{C}$, and by Cauchy-Schwarz:
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = (\|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|)^2" />

Taking square roots: $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$. $\blacksquare$

## Common Mistakes

⚠ **Forgetting the absolute value:** The inequality is $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$ — the inner product can be negative, but the right-hand side is always positive.

⚠ **Equality condition:** Equality in Cauchy-Schwarz holds iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent ($\mathbf{u} = c\mathbf{v}$ or $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$). Collinearity in $\mathbb{R}^n$ corresponds to $\cos\theta = \pm 1$.

⚠ **Triangle inequality direction:** The inequality $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$ also implies the **reverse triangle inequality** $|\|\mathbf{u}\|-\|\mathbf{v}\|| \leq \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|$, but these are different statements.

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="The Two Core Inequalities">

**Cauchy-Schwarz (Thm 18):** $|\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|$ — valid in any inner product space.

**Triangle inequality (Thm 19):** $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$ — follows from Cauchy-Schwarz.

**Cauchy-Schwarz instances:**
- $\mathbb{R}^n$: $(\sum u_iv_i)^2 \leq (\sum u_i^2)(\sum v_i^2)$
- $\mathbb{C}^n$: $|\sum u_i\overline{v_i}|^2 \leq (\sum|u_i|^2)(\sum|v_i|^2)$
- $L^2[a,b]$: $(\int fg)^2 \leq (\int f^2)(\int g^2)$

**Angle in inner product spaces:** Cauchy-Schwarz justifies defining $\cos\theta = \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle/(\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|)$ since this ratio lies in $[-1,1]$ over $\mathbb{R}$.
</SummaryBox>
