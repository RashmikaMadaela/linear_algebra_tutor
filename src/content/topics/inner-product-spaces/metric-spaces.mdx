## Big Idea

A **metric** (distance function) is the most general way to talk about "closeness" in mathematics.
Inner products give norms; norms give metrics — but you can define a metric directly without going through a norm or inner product.
Understanding this hierarchy — inner product → norm → metric — clarifies exactly which properties are built in at each level and which must be assumed.

## Intuition

A metric is an abstraction of everyday distance.
Think of cities: the distance between any two cities is non-negative, is zero only if they're the same city, is symmetric (distance from A to B equals distance from B to A), and satisfies the triangle inequality (going via a detour isn't shorter).
Any function satisfying these four properties is a metric — on any set, not just vectors.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={18} title="Metric (Distance Function)">
A **metric** on a set $X$ is a function $d: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying, for all $x,y,z \in X$:

1. **Non-negativity:** $d(x,y) \geq 0$.
2. **Identity of indiscernibles:** $d(x,y) = 0 \iff x = y$.
3. **Symmetry:** $d(x,y) = d(y,x)$.
4. **Triangle inequality:** $d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)$.

A set $X$ with a metric $d$ is called a **metric space** $(X, d)$.
</DefinitionBox>

## The Hierarchy: Inner Product → Norm → Metric

Every inner product space is automatically a normed space, and every normed space is automatically a metric space.
The implications are **one-directional** — you need stronger structure to go left, not right.

**Step 1: Inner product induces a norm.**
<BM math="\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}" />
Norm axioms follow from inner product axioms (triangle inequality = Thm 19).

**Step 2: Norm induces a metric.**
<BM math="d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\|" />

**Verification:**
1. $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\| \geq 0$ ✓ (norm positive definiteness)
2. $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})=0 \iff \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|=0 \iff \mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0} \iff \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{v}$ ✓
3. $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})=\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|=\|-(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u})\|=|\text{-}1|\|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}\|=d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u})$ ✓
4. $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w})=\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{w}\|=\|(\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v})+(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w})\|\leq\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|+\|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}\|=d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})+d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w})$ ✓

**Full hierarchy diagram:**
<BM math="\underbrace{\text{Hilbert space}}_{\text{inner product} + \text{complete}} \subset \underbrace{\text{Banach space}}_{\text{norm} + \text{complete}} \subset \underbrace{\text{Normed space}}_{\text{norm}} \subset \underbrace{\text{Metric space}}_{\text{distance}} \subset \underbrace{\text{Topological space}}_{\text{open sets}}" />

## Examples of Metrics

| Metric Space | $d(x,y)$ | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{R}^n$ (Euclidean) | $\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|_2$ | From dot product |
| $\mathbb{R}^n$ (taxicab) | $\sum_i|x_i-y_i|$ | From $\ell^1$ norm, not inner product |
| $C[a,b]$ | $\max_{t\in[a,b]}|f(t)-g(t)|$ | Supremum metric |
| Discrete metric | $d(x,y)=1$ if $x\neq y$, $0$ if $x=y$ | Not from any norm |
| $\mathbb{R}$ | $|x-y|$ | Standard metric |
| Graph (shortest path) | graph distance | No vector structure needed |

The **discrete metric** shows that metric spaces are strictly more general than normed spaces — the discrete metric cannot come from any norm (since norms are homogeneous: $\|\alpha x\|=|\alpha|\|x\|$, but a discrete metric can't scale).

## Convergence and Topology

The metric defines **open balls** $B(x,r) = \{y : d(x,y) < r\}$, which generate the topology.
A sequence $\{x_n\}$ **converges** to $x$ in a metric space iff $d(x_n,x) \to 0$.
The notion of Cauchy sequence and completeness from the previous lesson are metric-space concepts.

## Why This Matters for Linear Algebra

- Asking whether a sequence of matrices "converges" requires a metric (often induced by the spectral norm or Frobenius norm).
- The QR algorithm (Section VI) generates a sequence of matrices converging to triangular form — convergence is a metric-space statement.
- The SVD gives the best rank-$k$ approximation to a matrix — "best" means smallest distance in the Frobenius metric.
- Iterative methods (power iteration, etc.) converge in the operator-norm metric.

## Common Mistakes

⚠ **A metric need not come from a norm:** The taxicab and discrete metrics are valid metrics but cannot be expressed as $\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|$ for any norm.

⚠ **A norm need not come from an inner product:** The $\ell^1$ norm is a valid norm but fails the parallelogram law.

⚠ **The hierarchy is strict:** There exist metric spaces that are not normed (no linear structure), normed spaces that are not inner product spaces, and inner product spaces that are not Hilbert spaces (incomplete).

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Metric Spaces & the Hierarchy — Def 18">

**Def 18:** Metric $d: X\times X\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfying: non-negative, $d(x,y)=0\Leftrightarrow x=y$, symmetric, triangle inequality.

**Norm → metric:** $d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|$.

**Inner product → norm:** $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$.

**Full hierarchy (each $\subset$ the next):**
$$\text{Hilbert} \subset \text{Banach} \subset \text{Normed} \subset \text{Metric} \subset \text{Topological}$$

**Key takeaway:** Every inner product space is a metric space, but the converse fails at each step. The hierarchy clarifies exactly what structure is needed for each theorem.
</SummaryBox>
