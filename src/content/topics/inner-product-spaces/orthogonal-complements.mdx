## Big Idea

Given a subspace $W$ of an inner product space $V$, the **orthogonal complement** $W^\perp$ captures everything in $V$ that is orthogonal to $W$.
The central theorem is that $V$ **splits** into $W$ and $W^\perp$: every vector in $V$ can be written uniquely as a sum of a vector in $W$ and a vector in $W^\perp$.
The $W$-component of this decomposition is the **best approximation** (orthogonal projection) of the original vector by an element of $W$ — it minimizes the distance.

## Intuition

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, take $W$ to be a plane through the origin. The orthogonal complement $W^\perp$ is the line through the origin that is perpendicular to this plane.
Every vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ decomposes uniquely into a part lying in the plane and a part pointing along that perpendicular line.
The "shadow" of the vector onto the plane (found by dropping a perpendicular) is the **projection** $P\mathbf{u}$ — and this shadow is the closest point in $W$ to $\mathbf{u}$.

This geometric picture extends to any inner product space, including function spaces.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={16} title="Orthogonal Complement">
Let $W$ be a subspace of an inner product space $V$. The **orthogonal complement** of $W$ is:
<BM math="W^\perp = \{\mathbf{v} \in V \mid \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle = 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{w} \in W\}" />

$W^\perp$ is the set of all vectors in $V$ that are orthogonal to **every** vector in $W$.
</DefinitionBox>

## Main Theorem

<TheoremBox number={22} title="Orthogonal Decomposition & Best Approximation" proofSketch={<>Existence of decomposition: use an orthonormal basis $\{e_1,\ldots,e_k\}$ for $W$ (by Gram-Schmidt) and define $P\mathbf{u} = \sum_j \langle\mathbf{u},e_j\rangle e_j$. Then $\mathbf{u} - P\mathbf{u}$ is orthogonal to all of $W$. Uniqueness follows from the fact that if $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{w}'=\tilde\mathbf{w}+\tilde\mathbf{w}'$ then $\mathbf{w}-\tilde\mathbf{w}\in W\cap W^\perp = \{\mathbf{0}\}$. Best approximation: $\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}-P\mathbf{u}\|^2+\|P\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{w}\|^2 \geq \|\mathbf{u}-P\mathbf{u}\|^2$ by Pythagoras.</>}>

Let $W$ be a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space $V$. Then:

1. **$W^\perp$ is a subspace of $V$.**

2. **Direct sum decomposition:** Every $\mathbf{u} \in V$ can be written **uniquely** as:
<BM math="\mathbf{u} = P\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}^\perp, \quad P\mathbf{u} \in W,\quad \mathbf{u}^\perp \in W^\perp" />
We write $V = W \oplus W^\perp$.

3. **Best approximation:** If $\{e_1,\ldots,e_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $W$, then:
<BM math="P\mathbf{u} = \sum_{j=1}^k \langle\mathbf{u}, e_j\rangle\, e_j" />
Moreover, $P\mathbf{u}$ is the **unique closest point** in $W$ to $\mathbf{u}$:
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u} - P\mathbf{u}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{w}\| \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{w} \in W" />
with equality iff $\mathbf{w} = P\mathbf{u}$.
</TheoremBox>

**Why $W^\perp$ is a subspace:** If $\langle\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{w}\rangle=0$ and $\langle\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{w}\rangle=0$ for all $\mathbf{w}\in W$, then $\langle\alpha\mathbf{v}_1+\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{w}\rangle = \alpha\langle\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{w}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{w}\rangle = 0$. ✓

**Pythagoras in inner product spaces:** The decomposition $\mathbf{u} = P\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}^\perp$ with $P\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{u}^\perp$ gives:
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 = \|P\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}^\perp\|^2" />

## Connection to Least Squares

The best approximation theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is exactly **least squares regression**:
- $W = $ column space of matrix $A$ (set of all $A\mathbf{x}$)
- $\mathbf{u} = $ the target vector $\mathbf{b}$
- $P\mathbf{b} = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$ = the least-squares solution
- Normal equations: $A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}$

## Common Mistakes

⚠ **$W^\perp$ depends on the whole space $V$, not just $W$:** The same subspace $W$ has a different complement in different ambient spaces.

⚠ **$(W^\perp)^\perp = W$ only in finite dimensions:** In infinite-dimensional spaces, the double complement may be larger than $W$ unless $W$ is closed.

⚠ **Projection formula requires orthonormal basis:** The formula $P\mathbf{u} = \sum_j\langle\mathbf{u},e_j\rangle e_j$ only works when $\{e_j\}$ is **orthonormal**, not just orthogonal.

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Orthogonal Complements — Key Facts">

**Def 16:** $W^\perp = \{\mathbf{v} : \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle=0\;\forall\,\mathbf{w}\in W\}$ — always a subspace.

**Thm 22:** $V = W \oplus W^\perp$. Every $\mathbf{u} = P\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}^\perp$ uniquely.

**Projection formula** (orthonormal basis $\{e_j\}$ for $W$):
$$P\mathbf{u} = \sum_{j=1}^k \langle\mathbf{u},e_j\rangle\,e_j$$

**Best approximation:** $P\mathbf{u}$ is the closest point in $W$ to $\mathbf{u}$.

**Pythagoras:** $\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 = \|P\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}^\perp\|^2$.
</SummaryBox>
