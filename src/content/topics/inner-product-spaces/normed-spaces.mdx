## Big Idea

A **norm** is an abstraction of "length" — but it is more general than the induced norm of an inner product.
Some normed spaces don't come from any inner product at all (e.g., the $L^1$ norm).
When a normed space is also **complete** (every Cauchy sequence converges), it is a **Banach space**.
When a complete normed space comes from an inner product, it is a **Hilbert space** — the richest structure in functional analysis, and the setting for quantum mechanics and $L^2$ function theory.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={17} title="Norm on a Vector Space">
A **norm** on a vector space $V$ over $F$ ($\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$) is a function $\|\cdot\|: V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying, for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v} \in V$ and $\alpha \in F$:

1. **Positive definiteness:** $\|\mathbf{v}\| \geq 0$, with $\|\mathbf{v}\|=0$ iff $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$.
2. **Absolute homogeneity:** $\|\alpha\mathbf{v}\| = |\alpha|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|$.
3. **Triangle inequality:** $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|+\|\mathbf{v}\|$.

A vector space with a norm is called a **normed space** $(V,\|\cdot\|)$.
</DefinitionBox>

**Every inner product induces a norm** via $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$.
(Axioms 1 and 2 are immediate; axiom 3 = Triangle inequality = Theorem 19.)
But not every norm comes from an inner product.

**Test (Parallelogram law):** A norm $\|\cdot\|$ arises from an inner product iff for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}$:
<BM math="\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2" />

## Common Norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$

| Name | Formula | Comes from inner product? |
|------|---------|--------------------------|
| Euclidean ($\ell^2$) | $\|\mathbf{v}\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum v_i^2}$ | Yes (dot product) |
| Taxicab ($\ell^1$) | $\|\mathbf{v}\|_1 = \sum |v_i|$ | No |
| Maximum ($\ell^\infty$) | $\|\mathbf{v}\|_\infty = \max_i |v_i|$ | No |
| $\ell^p$, $p\geq 1$ | $\|\mathbf{v}\|_p = (\sum |v_i|^p)^{1/p}$ | Only $p=2$ |

## Note 7 — Completeness, Banach & Hilbert Spaces

### Cauchy Sequences

A sequence $\{\mathbf{v}_n\}$ in a normed space $(V,\|\cdot\|)$ is a **Cauchy sequence** if:
<BM math="\forall\,\varepsilon > 0,\; \exists\, N \in \mathbb{N}: \quad m,n > N \implies \|\mathbf{v}_m - \mathbf{v}_n\| < \varepsilon" />

Informally: the terms get arbitrarily close together.
Every convergent sequence is Cauchy — but in a **general** normed space, a Cauchy sequence might not converge (its "limit" might not exist in the space).

**Example of failure:** Consider $\mathbb{Q}$ with $|x|$ norm. The sequence $3, 3.1, 3.14, 3.141, \ldots$ (rational approximations to $\pi$) is Cauchy but converges to $\pi\notin\mathbb{Q}$.

### Complete Spaces

A normed space $(V,\|\cdot\|)$ is **complete** if every Cauchy sequence in $V$ converges to a limit in $V$.

### Banach Space

A **complete normed space** is called a **Banach space**.

**Examples:**
- $\mathbb{R}^n$ with any norm — complete ✓
- $C[a,b]$ with $\|f\|_\infty = \max|f(x)|$ — complete (Banach) ✓
- $C[a,b]$ with $\|f\|_2 = \sqrt{\int_a^b f^2\,dx}$ — **not** complete (limits of Cauchy sequences of continuous functions may be discontinuous)

### Hilbert Space

A **Hilbert space** is a complete inner product space — i.e., a Banach space whose norm comes from an inner product.

**Examples:**
- $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathbb{C}^n$ — finite-dimensional, trivially complete ✓
- $L^2[a,b] = \{f : \int_a^b |f|^2 < \infty\}$ with $\langle f,g\rangle = \int_a^b f\bar{g}\,dx$ — the most important infinite-dimensional Hilbert space ✓
- $\ell^2 = \{(x_1,x_2,\ldots) : \sum |x_i|^2 < \infty\}$ with $\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle = \sum x_i\bar{y}_i$ — the sequence space analogue ✓

### Hierarchy

<BM math="\text{Inner product space} \xrightarrow{\text{complete}} \text{Hilbert space}" />
<BM math="\text{Normed space} \xrightarrow{\text{complete}} \text{Banach space}" />
<BM math="\text{Hilbert space} \subset \text{Banach space} \subset \text{Normed space} \subset \text{Metric space}" />

Every Hilbert space is a Banach space; every Banach space is a normed space; but the inclusions are strict.

## Why Completeness Matters

In a Hilbert space, the **projection theorem** (Thm 22) holds in full generality — not just for finite-dimensional subspaces.
Completeness guarantees that the orthonormal expansion $\mathbf{u} = \sum_k \langle\mathbf{u},e_k\rangle e_k$ actually converges (in the $L^2$ sense) when $\{e_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis.
This is why Fourier series work in $L^2[0,2\pi]$ — it is a Hilbert space.

## Common Mistakes

⚠ **"Complete" ≠ "spans everything":** Completeness is about convergence of Cauchy sequences, not about algebraic spanning.

⚠ **$C[a,b]$ is Banach under $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ but not under $\|\cdot\|_2$:** The choice of norm matters. The same underlying set can be Banach with one norm and not with another.

⚠ **$L^2[a,b] \neq C[a,b]$:** $L^2$ consists of equivalence classes of measurable functions (two functions equal a.e. are identified), not just continuous ones.

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Norms, Banach & Hilbert Spaces — Note 7">

**Def 17:** Norm = positive definite + absolute homogeneity + triangle inequality.

**Cauchy sequence:** Terms get arbitrarily close — but convergence not guaranteed in incomplete spaces.

**Banach space:** Complete normed space.

**Hilbert space:** Complete inner product space (special Banach space).

**Parallelogram law** tests whether a norm comes from an inner product.

**Key spaces:**
- $\mathbb{R}^n$, $\mathbb{C}^n$: Hilbert ✓
- $L^2[a,b]$: Hilbert ✓ (the working space for Fourier series and quantum mechanics)
- $C[a,b]$ with $\|\cdot\|_\infty$: Banach (not Hilbert) ✓
- $C[a,b]$ with $\|\cdot\|_2$: neither Banach nor Hilbert ✗
</SummaryBox>
