## Big Idea

Vector spaces let us add vectors and scale them — but they say nothing about *length* or *angle*.
An **inner product** is the extra structure that gives a vector space geometry: it defines what it means for two vectors to be perpendicular, how long a vector is, and how to measure the "size" of a projection.
Every notion of orthogonality, projection, and best approximation that follows depends on this definition.

## Intuition

You already know the dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = u_1v_1+\cdots+u_nv_n$.
It has three key properties: it's linear in each argument, symmetric, and always gives $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u} \geq 0$ (zero only when $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}$).
An inner product is exactly an abstraction of these three properties — a rule that assigns a scalar to any two vectors in a way that behaves like the dot product.

Over $\mathbb{C}$, symmetry becomes **conjugate symmetry** ($\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = \overline{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle}$) so that $\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle$ is always real.
The resulting structure is called a **Hermitian inner product**.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={14} title="Inner Product (over an ordered field)">
Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $F$ (where $F = \mathbb{R}$ or $F = \mathbb{C}$).
An **inner product** on $V$ is a function $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle: V \times V \to F$ satisfying, for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \in V$ and $\alpha \in F$:

1. **Linearity in the first argument:**
<BM math="\langle\alpha\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle = \alpha\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle + \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle" />

2. **Conjugate symmetry:**
<BM math="\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = \overline{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle}" />

(Over $\mathbb{R}$, this is just $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle$ — ordinary symmetry.)

3. **Positive definiteness:**
<BM math="\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle \geq 0, \quad \text{with equality iff } \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}" />

A vector space with an inner product is called an **inner product space**.

**Induced norm:** The inner product defines a norm (length) by $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$.
</DefinitionBox>

**Note on linearity in the second argument:** Combining conjugate symmetry with linearity in the first argument gives:
<BM math="\langle\mathbf{u}, \alpha\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\rangle = \bar{\alpha}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle" />
(conjugate-linear in the second argument over $\mathbb{C}$; linear over $\mathbb{R}$). This is Theorem 17.

<TheoremBox number={17} title="Conjugate-Linearity in the Second Argument" proofSketch={<>Use conjugate symmetry to flip each term, apply linearity in the first argument, then flip back: <M math="\langle\mathbf{u},\alpha\mathbf{v}\rangle = \overline{\langle\alpha\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle} = \overline{\alpha\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle} = \bar{\alpha}\overline{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle} = \bar{\alpha}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle" />.</>}>

For all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \in V$ and $\alpha \in F$:

<BM math="\langle\mathbf{u}, \alpha\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}\rangle = \bar{\alpha}\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle + \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle" />

In particular: $\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{0}\rangle = 0$ and $\langle\mathbf{0},\mathbf{v}\rangle = 0$ for all $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}$.
</TheoremBox>

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Inner Products — Key Facts">

**Three axioms:** linearity (first slot), conjugate symmetry, positive definiteness.

**Over $\mathbb{R}$:** conjugate symmetry = ordinary symmetry; second slot is also linear → **bilinear form**.

**Over $\mathbb{C}$:** second slot is conjugate-linear → **sesquilinear form** (Thm 17).

**Induced norm:** $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}$ — every inner product space is automatically a normed space.

**Key examples:**
| Space | Inner Product | Norm |
|-------|--------------|------|
| $\mathbb{R}^n$ | $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \sum u_iv_i$ | Euclidean $\|\mathbf{u}\|_2$ |
| $\mathbb{C}^n$ | $\sum u_i\overline{v_i}$ | $\sqrt{\sum\|u_i\|^2}$ |
| $C[a,b]$ | $\int_a^b fg\,dx$ | $L^2$-norm |
| $M_{n\times n}$ | $\operatorname{tr}(B^TA)$ | Frobenius norm |
</SummaryBox>
