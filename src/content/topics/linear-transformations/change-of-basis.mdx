## Big Idea

The same linear map $T: V \to V$ can be represented by different matrices depending on which basis you use. Understanding how these matrices relate — and choosing a basis that makes the matrix as simple as possible — is the bridge to eigenvalue theory and canonical forms.

---

## Transition (Change-of-Basis) Matrix

<DefinitionBox number={21} title="Transition Matrix">
Let $\mathcal{B} = (\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n)$ and $\mathcal{B}' = (\mathbf{b}'_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}'_n)$ be two ordered bases for a vector space $V$.

The **transition matrix** (or **change-of-basis matrix**) **from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{B}'$** is the $n \times n$ matrix:

$$P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}} = \Big([\mathbf{b}_1]_{\mathcal{B}'} \;\Big|\; [\mathbf{b}_2]_{\mathcal{B}'} \;\Big|\; \cdots \;\Big|\; [\mathbf{b}_n]_{\mathcal{B}'}\Big)$$

The $j$-th column is the coordinate vector of the **old** basis vector $\mathbf{b}_j$ **in the new basis** $\mathcal{B}'$.

**Key property:**
$$[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}\, [\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B} \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{v} \in V$$
</DefinitionBox>

---

## Properties of Transition Matrices

<TheoremBox number={26} title="Transition Matrices are Invertible" proofSketch={<>The map v ↦ [v]_B is an isomorphism V→Fⁿ (coordinate map). So is v ↦ [v]_{B'}. The transition matrix is the composition of one isomorphism with the inverse of another, hence a linear isomorphism Fⁿ→Fⁿ, hence invertible. Its inverse is the transition matrix in the other direction.</>}>
1. $P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}$ is invertible, with $(P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}})^{-1} = P_{\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{B}'}$.
2. **Chain rule:** $P_{\mathcal{B}'' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}} = P_{\mathcal{B}'' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}'}\, P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}$
3. For $\mathbb{F}^n$ with standard basis $\mathcal{E}$: $P_{\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{E}}$ is the matrix whose columns are the basis vectors of $\mathcal{B}$.
</TheoremBox>

## Similarity: How the Matrix of T Changes

<TheoremBox number={27} title="Change-of-Basis Formula for Linear Maps" proofSketch={<>For any v∈V: [T(v)]_{B'} = [T]_{B',B'} [v]_{B'}. Also [T(v)]_{B'} = P [T(v)]_B = P [T]_{B,B} [v]_B = P [T]_{B,B} P⁻¹ [v]_{B'}. Since this holds for all [v]_{B'}, we get [T]_{B',B'} = P [T]_{B,B} P⁻¹.</>}>
Let $T: V \to V$ be linear, $\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{B}'$ two ordered bases for $V$, and $P = P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}$.

Then the matrix representations of $T$ in the two bases are related by:

$$[T]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P\, [T]_\mathcal{B}\, P^{-1}$$

Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are called **similar** if there exists an invertible $P$ with $B = PAP^{-1}$.

**Similar matrices represent the same linear map in different bases.**
</TheoremBox>

---

## Similarity Invariants

Since similar matrices represent the same linear map, they share all **similarity invariants**:

| Invariant | Why preserved |
|:---|:---|
| Eigenvalues | $\det(PAP^{-1} - \lambda I) = \det(A - \lambda I)$ |
| Determinant | $\det(PAP^{-1}) = \det A$ |
| Trace | $\operatorname{tr}(PAP^{-1}) = \operatorname{tr}(A)$ |
| Rank | $\operatorname{rank}(PAP^{-1}) = \operatorname{rank}(A)$ |
| Characteristic polynomial | Same as eigenvalues argument |

## The Bridge to Eigenvalues

The central question of Section IV is: **can we choose a basis $\mathcal{B}$ so that $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ is diagonal?**

This is possible when $T$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. The change-of-basis formula tells us exactly how to diagonalize: form $P$ from eigenvectors as columns, and $P^{-1}AP = D$ (diagonal).

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Change of Basis">
- Transition matrix $P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}$: columns are old basis vectors expressed in new basis.
- $[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P\, [\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B}$: multiplying by $P$ converts old coordinates to new.
- Change-of-basis for linear maps: $[T]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P[T]_\mathcal{B} P^{-1}$ (similarity transformation).
- Similar matrices share: eigenvalues, determinant, trace, rank, characteristic polynomial.
- Goal of eigenvalue theory: choose $\mathcal{B}$ (eigenbasis) to make $[T]_\mathcal{B}$ diagonal.
- In $\mathbb{F}^n$: $P_{\mathcal{B}' \leftarrow \mathcal{B}} = (B')^{-1} B$ where $B, B'$ are matrices of basis vectors.
</SummaryBox>
