## Big Idea

The linear map stays the same, but its matrix changes when the basis changes.
That change is controlled by an invertible transition matrix.

This is the algebraic bridge between coordinates and geometry.

## Intuition

Coordinates are labels, not the object itself.
If you rotate or reshape your coordinate axes, vectors and linear maps are unchanged, but their coordinate columns and matrices change.

Change of basis is exactly the rule for translating between these coordinate descriptions.

## Transition Matrix

<DefinitionBox number={21} title="Transition Matrix">
Given two ordered bases, the transition matrix converts coordinate columns from one basis to the other.

If $P$ converts coordinates from old basis to new basis, then

<BM math="[v]_{new} = P [v]_{old}" />

for every vector $v$.
</DefinitionBox>

Important direction convention:

- $P$ multiplies old coordinates and returns new coordinates.
- $P^{-1}$ does the reverse conversion.

## Change Of Matrix Formula

<TheoremBox number={26} title="Transition Matrix Is Invertible">
If $P$ converts coordinates from old basis to new basis, then $P$ is invertible and $P^{-1}$ converts coordinates from new basis back to old basis.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={27} title="Basis Change For A Linear Map">
Let $A$ be the matrix of $T$ in the old basis. If $P$ is the transition matrix from old to new coordinates, then the new matrix is

<BM math="A_{new} = P A P^{-1}" />

So the two matrices are similar.
</TheoremBox>

This can be read as three steps:

1. Convert new coordinates to old using $P^{-1}$.
2. Apply the old-basis matrix $A$.
3. Convert back to new coordinates using $P$.

## Invariants Under Similarity

If two matrices are related by $A_{new} = P A P^{-1}$, they have the same:

- eigenvalues
- determinant
- trace
- rank
- characteristic polynomial

So when two matrices are similar, they represent the same linear transformation under different coordinate systems.

## Worked Mini Example

Take reflection across the line $y=x$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

- In standard basis its matrix is

<BM math="A = [[0,1],[1,0]]" />

- In basis $(1,1),(1,-1)$ the matrix becomes diagonal:

<BM math="A_{new} = [[1,0],[0,-1]]" />

Both matrices describe the same map, and are related by similarity.

## Why It Matters

Diagonalization is a basis change where the new basis is made of eigenvectors.
In that basis, the matrix is diagonal and computations become much simpler.

This is why basis choice is a computational strategy, not just notation.

## Common Mistakes

- Mixing the direction of $P$ and $P^{-1}$.
- Treating equal matrices and similar matrices as the same concept.
- Forgetting that basis order matters.

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Change of Basis">
- Transition matrix changes coordinates.
- Matrix of a map changes by similarity.
- Similar matrices represent one linear map in different bases.
- Choosing a good basis simplifies the matrix.
- The formula is $A_{new}=PAP^{-1}$ with direction carefully tracked.
</SummaryBox>
