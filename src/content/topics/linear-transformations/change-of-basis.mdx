## Big Idea

The linear map stays the same, but its matrix changes when the basis changes.
That change is controlled by an invertible transition matrix.

## Transition Matrix

<DefinitionBox number={21} title="Transition Matrix">
Given two ordered bases, the transition matrix converts coordinate columns from one basis to the other.

If $P$ converts coordinates from old basis to new basis, then

<BM math="[v]_{new} = P [v]_{old}" />

for every vector $v$.
</DefinitionBox>

## Change Of Matrix Formula

<TheoremBox number={26} title="Basis Change For A Linear Map">
Let $A$ be the matrix of $T$ in the old basis. If $P$ is the transition matrix from old to new coordinates, then the new matrix is

<BM math="A_{new} = P A P^{-1}" />

So the two matrices are similar.
</TheoremBox>

## Invariants Under Similarity

If two matrices are related by $A_{new} = P A P^{-1}$, they have the same:

- eigenvalues
- determinant
- trace
- rank
- characteristic polynomial

## Why It Matters

Diagonalization is a basis change where the new basis is made of eigenvectors.
In that basis, the matrix is diagonal and computations become much simpler.

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Change of Basis">
- Transition matrix changes coordinates.
- Matrix of a map changes by similarity.
- Similar matrices represent one linear map in different bases.
- Choosing a good basis simplifies the matrix.
</SummaryBox>
