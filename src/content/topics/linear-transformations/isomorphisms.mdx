## Big Idea

An **isomorphism** is a bijective linear map ŌĆö one that is both injective and surjective. When an isomorphism exists between two vector spaces, they are structurally identical: everything that is true of one (in terms of vector space structure) is true of the other. The surprising punchline: **two finite-dimensional spaces are isomorphic if and only if they have the same dimension**.

---

## Isomorphisms

<DefinitionBox number={20} title="Isomorphism and Automorphism">
Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over $\mathbb{F}$.

- A **linear isomorphism** is a bijective linear map $T: V \to W$.
- We write $V \cong W$ and say $V$ and $W$ are **isomorphic** as vector spaces.
- An **automorphism** of $V$ is an isomorphism $T: V \to V$ (a self-isomorphism). The set of all automorphisms $\operatorname{Aut}(V)$ forms a group under composition.
</DefinitionBox>

## Inverse of an Isomorphism

<TheoremBox number={24} title="Inverse of an Isomorphism is Linear" proofSketch={<>Let T: VŌåÆW be an isomorphism, TŌü╗┬╣: WŌåÆV the set-theoretic inverse. For wŌéü,wŌééŌłłW, let vŌéü=TŌü╗┬╣(wŌéü), vŌéé=TŌü╗┬╣(wŌéé). Then T(╬▒vŌéü+╬▓vŌéé)=╬▒wŌéü+╬▓wŌéé, so TŌü╗┬╣(╬▒wŌéü+╬▓wŌéé)=╬▒vŌéü+╬▓vŌéé=╬▒TŌü╗┬╣(wŌéü)+╬▓TŌü╗┬╣(wŌéé). Ō£ō</>}>
If $T: V \to W$ is an isomorphism, then $T^{-1}: W \to V$ is also a linear isomorphism.

Consequently, $\cong$ is an **equivalence relation** on vector spaces:
- Reflexive: $V \cong V$ (via $I_V$)
- Symmetric: $V \cong W \implies W \cong V$
- Transitive: $V \cong W$ and $W \cong U \implies V \cong U$
</TheoremBox>

## The Classification Theorem

<TheoremBox number={25} title="Classification of Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces" proofSketch={<>(ŌćÆ) If T: VŌåÆW is an isomorphism and B is a basis for V, then T(B) is a basis for W (T(B) is LI since T is injective, and spans W since T is surjective). So dim W = |T(B)| = |B| = dim V. (ŌćÉ) Let dim V = dim W = n. Let B=(bŌéü,ŌĆ”,bŌéÖ) be a basis for V and C=(cŌéü,ŌĆ”,cŌéÖ) for W. Define T(╬Ż╬▒ßĄóbßĄó)=╬Ż╬▒ßĄócßĄó. This is well-defined (unique representations), linear, injective (ker={0}), and surjective (T maps onto all of W since C spans W). ŌłÄ</>}>
Two **finite-dimensional** vector spaces $V$ and $W$ over $\mathbb{F}$ are isomorphic if and only if:

$$V \cong W \iff \dim V = \dim W$$

**Corollary:** Every $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{F}^n$.
This means $\mathbb{F}^n$ is the "universal model" of all $n$-dimensional spaces over $\mathbb{F}$.
</TheoremBox>

## Isomorphism Invariants

Any property preserved by isomorphisms is called an **isomorphism invariant**. Key invariants:

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Isomorphisms">
- An isomorphism is a bijective linear map; its inverse is also linear.
- $V \cong W \iff \dim V = \dim W$ (for finite-dim spaces over the same field).
- Every $n$-dim space is isomorphic to $\mathbb{F}^n$ ŌĆö the universal model.
- $\det[T] \neq 0$ (for an endomorphism) $\iff$ $T$ is an isomorphism.
- Isomorphic spaces are structurally identical; all vector space properties transfer.
- Automorphisms of $V$ form a group $\operatorname{Aut}(V)$ under composition.
</SummaryBox>
