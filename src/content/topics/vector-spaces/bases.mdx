## Big Idea

A **basis** is a set of vectors that does two things at once: it spans the space (every vector is reachable) and it is linearly independent (no redundancy). A basis gives every vector a unique "address" in coordinates.

---

## Definitions

<DefinitionBox number={9} title="Basis (Finite-Dimensional Case)">
Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$. A finite set $\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\} \subseteq V$ is a **basis** for $V$ if:

1. $\mathcal{B}$ is **linearly independent**, and
2. $\operatorname{span}(\mathcal{B}) = V$ (i.e., $\mathcal{B}$ **spans** $V$)

Equivalently: $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis iff every vector $\mathbf{v} \in V$ can be written as a **unique** linear combination of basis vectors:
$$\mathbf{v} = \alpha_1\mathbf{b}_1 + \alpha_2\mathbf{b}_2 + \cdots + \alpha_n\mathbf{b}_n, \quad \alpha_i \in \mathbb{F} \text{ uniquely determined}$$
</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox number={10} title="Hamel Basis (Infinite-Dimensional Case)">
For a general (possibly infinite-dimensional) vector space $V$, a **Hamel basis** (or **algebraic basis**) is a subset $\mathcal{B} \subseteq V$ such that:

1. $\mathcal{B}$ is linearly independent (every **finite** subset is LI), and
2. Every $\mathbf{v} \in V$ is a **finite** linear combination of elements of $\mathcal{B}$

The key word is *finite*: even if $\mathcal{B}$ is infinite, representations involve only finitely many non-zero coefficients.
</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox number={13} title="Schauder Basis (Topological Vector Spaces)">
In an infinite-dimensional **topological** vector space (where convergence is defined), a **Schauder basis** is a sequence $\{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots\}$ such that every vector has a unique representation as a **convergent infinite series**:
$$\mathbf{v} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \alpha_n \mathbf{b}_n$$

**Example:** In $L^2[-\pi, \pi]$, the functions $\{1, \cos(nx), \sin(nx)\}_{n \geq 1}$ form a Schauder basis — this is the Fourier series.

> **Warning:** Hamel basis ≠ Schauder basis. Every Hamel basis is LI in the algebraic sense, but a Schauder basis may be LD over finite linear combinations — its power comes from infinite series.
</DefinitionBox>

## Basis Extension and Reduction Theorems

<TheoremBox number={9} title="Invariance of Basis Size" proofSketch={<>If B₁ and B₂ are two finite bases for V, and |B₁| = m, |B₂| = n. Since B₁ spans V and B₂ is LI, by the Steinitz exchange lemma, m ≥ n. By symmetry, n ≥ m. Hence m = n.</>}>
Any two (Hamel) bases for the same vector space have the same **cardinality** (number of elements). This common cardinality is called the **dimension** of $V$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={10} title="Every LI Set Extends to a Basis" proofSketch={<>If S is LI and doesn't span V, pick v ∉ span(S). Then S ∪ {v} is LI (Theorem 8). Repeat (or use Zorn's Lemma for the infinite case) until spanning is achieved.</>}>
Let $V$ be a vector space and $S \subseteq V$ a linearly independent subset. Then there exists a basis $\mathcal{B}$ for $V$ with $S \subseteq \mathcal{B}$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={11} title="Every Spanning Set Reduces to a Basis" proofSketch={<>If S spans V and is LD, some vector v ∈ S lies in the span of the rest (Theorem 7). Remove it — the remaining set still spans. Repeat until LI.</>}>
Let $V$ be a vector space and $S \subseteq V$ a spanning set. Then there exists a basis $\mathcal{B}$ for $V$ with $\mathcal{B} \subseteq S$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={12} title="Steinitz Exchange Lemma" proofSketch={<>By induction on k. For k=1: if v₁ ∉ span(B), contradiction since B spans V. So v₁ = Σαᵢbᵢ with some αⱼ ≠ 0. Then bⱼ = αⱼ⁻¹(v₁ - Σᵢ≠ⱼαᵢbᵢ), so replacing bⱼ with v₁ still spans V. Continue inductively.</>}>
Let $\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}$ be a basis for $V$ and $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ be a linearly independent subset. Then $k \leq n$, and we can replace $k$ of the vectors in $\mathcal{B}$ by $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ to get another basis for $V$.
</TheoremBox>

## Uniqueness of Coordinates

A key consequence of LI + spanning is uniqueness of representation:

> If $\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}$ is a basis for $V$, then every $\mathbf{v} \in V$ has a **unique** representation $\mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \mathbf{b}_i$. The scalars $\alpha_i$ are called the **coordinates** of $\mathbf{v}$ with respect to $\mathcal{B}$.

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Bases">
- A **basis** is an LI spanning set. Equivalently, it gives unique coordinates to every vector.
- **Hamel basis**: algebraic basis, all combinations finite — even for infinite-dim spaces.
- **Schauder basis**: allows convergent infinite series — for topological vector spaces.
- Any LI set can be extended to a basis; any spanning set can be reduced to a basis.
- All bases for the same space have the same size (cardinality = dimension, next lesson).
- Standard bases: $\mathbf{e}_i$ for $\mathbb{F}^n$; $1, x, \ldots, x^n$ for $\mathbb{P}_n$; $E_{ij}$ for $M_{m\times n}$.
</SummaryBox>
