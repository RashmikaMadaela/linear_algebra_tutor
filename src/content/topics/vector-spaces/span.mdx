## Big Idea

Given a set of vectors, you can combine them with arbitrary scalars to make infinitely many new vectors. The collection of *all* such combinations forms a subspace — the **span** of the set. Span is the fundamental tool for describing what a set of vectors "generates."

---

## Linear Combinations

<DefinitionBox number={6} title="Linear Combination">
Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ and let $S = \{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\} \subseteq V$. A **linear combination** of the vectors in $S$ is any expression of the form:

$$\alpha_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + \alpha_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + \alpha_k \mathbf{v}_k$$

where $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_k \in \mathbb{F}$ are scalars called the **coefficients**.
</DefinitionBox>

**Examples:**
- In $\mathbb{R}^3$: $3(1,0,0) - 2(0,1,0) + 5(0,0,1) = (3,-2,5)$ is a linear combination of the standard basis vectors.
- In $\mathbb{P}_2(\mathbb{R})$: $2(x^2) - 3(x) + 1(1) = 2x^2 - 3x + 1$ is a linear combination of $\{1, x, x^2\}$.

---

## Span

<DefinitionBox number={7} title="Span">
Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ and $S \subseteq V$ (possibly infinite). The **span** of $S$ is the set of **all finite** linear combinations of elements of $S$:

$$\operatorname{span}(S) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_i \mathbf{v}_i \;\middle|\; k \in \mathbb{N},\; \mathbf{v}_i \in S,\; \alpha_i \in \mathbb{F} \right\}$$

**Special cases:**
- $\operatorname{span}(\varnothing) = \{\mathbf{0}\}$ (the span of the empty set is the zero subspace)
- If $S = V$, we say $S$ **spans** $V$ or is a **spanning set** for $V$
</DefinitionBox>

---

## Span is the Smallest Subspace

<TheoremBox number={6} title="Span is the Smallest Subspace Containing S" proofSketch={<>First show span(S) is a subspace: it contains 𝟎 (empty combination), and is closed under linear combinations (concatenate coefficient lists). Then show minimality: if W is any subspace containing S, and v = Σαᵢvᵢ ∈ span(S), then each vᵢ ∈ S ⊆ W, and W is closed under linear combinations, so v ∈ W. Hence span(S) ⊆ W.</>}>
For any subset $S$ of a vector space $V$:

1. $\operatorname{span}(S)$ is a subspace of $V$.
2. $S \subseteq \operatorname{span}(S)$ (each vector in $S$ is in its own span, with coefficient 1).
3. If $W$ is any subspace of $V$ with $S \subseteq W$, then $\operatorname{span}(S) \subseteq W$.

In other words, $\operatorname{span}(S)$ is the **smallest subspace of $V$ that contains $S$**.
</TheoremBox>

## Spanning and Systems of Equations

> **Key connection:** Asking whether $\mathbf{b} \in \operatorname{span}(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\})$ is the same as asking whether the system $[A | \mathbf{b}]$ is consistent, where $A = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_k]$. This is why row reduction is central to linear algebra.

$$\mathbf{b} \in \operatorname{span}(\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k) \iff A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \text{ is consistent}$$

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Span and Linear Combinations">
- A **linear combination** is a sum $\alpha_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + \alpha_k\mathbf{v}_k$ with scalars $\alpha_i \in \mathbb{F}$.
- The **span** of a set $S$ is the collection of all finite linear combinations — always a subspace.
- $\operatorname{span}(S)$ is the **smallest** subspace containing $S$.
- $S$ spans $V$ iff every vector in $V$ can be written as a linear combination of elements of $S$.
- Testing membership in span ↔ testing consistency of a linear system.
- $\operatorname{span}(\varnothing) = \{\mathbf{0}\}$.
</SummaryBox>
