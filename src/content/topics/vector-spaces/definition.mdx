## Big Idea

A **vector space** is any set where you can add elements together and scale them by numbers — and where those two operations behave "sensibly." The real power is that vectors don't have to be arrows in $\mathbb{R}^n$: polynomials, matrices, functions, and continuous signals can all be vector spaces.

## Intuition

Think of $\mathbb{R}^2$: you can add two arrows tip-to-tail, and you can stretch or shrink an arrow by a scalar. Those operations satisfy obvious rules — adding zero does nothing, reversing a vector undoes it, scaling by $1$ does nothing. A vector space is just an *abstraction* of exactly those rules, so they can apply to any collection of mathematical objects.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={4} title="Vector Space">
Let $\mathbb{F}$ be a field. A **vector space** over $\mathbb{F}$ is a non-empty set $V$ equipped with two operations:

- **Vector addition**: $+: V \times V \to V$
- **Scalar multiplication**: $\cdot: \mathbb{F} \times V \to V$

satisfying the following **ten axioms**. For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in V$ and all $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{F}$:

**Axioms for addition ($V, +$ is an abelian group):**
1. **Closure**: $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in V$
2. **Associativity**: $(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) + \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$
3. **Zero vector**: $\exists\, \mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $\mathbf{0} + \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$
4. **Additive inverse**: $\exists\, (-\mathbf{v}) \in V$ such that $\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$
5. **Commutativity**: $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}$

**Axioms for scalar multiplication:**
6. **Closure**: $\alpha \mathbf{v} \in V$
7. **Distributivity over vectors**: $\alpha(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = \alpha\mathbf{u} + \alpha\mathbf{v}$
8. **Distributivity over scalars**: $(\alpha + \beta)\mathbf{v} = \alpha\mathbf{v} + \beta\mathbf{v}$
9. **Associativity of scaling**: $(\alpha\beta)\mathbf{v} = \alpha(\beta\mathbf{v})$
10. **Unit scalar**: $1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$, where $1$ is the multiplicative identity in $\mathbb{F}$

Elements of $V$ are called **vectors**; elements of $\mathbb{F}$ are called **scalars**.
</DefinitionBox>

## Key Consequences

<TheoremBox number={4} title="Basic Vector Space Properties" proofSketch={<>Each part follows from the axioms. For (i): <M math="0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = (0+0)\mathbf{v} = 0\mathbf{v} + 0\mathbf{v}" />; subtract <M math="0\mathbf{v}" /> from both sides. For (ii): <M math="\alpha \mathbf{0} = \alpha(\mathbf{0}+\mathbf{0}) = \alpha\mathbf{0} + \alpha\mathbf{0}" />; subtract. For (iii): <M math="(-1)\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{v} = (-1)\mathbf{v} + 1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = (-1+1)\mathbf{v} = 0\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}" />.</>}>
Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$. Then for all $\mathbf{v} \in V$ and $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}$:

1. $0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ (scalar zero times any vector is the zero vector)
2. $\alpha \cdot \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$ (any scalar times the zero vector is the zero vector)
3. $(-1)\mathbf{v} = -\mathbf{v}$ (multiplying by $-1$ gives the additive inverse)
4. If $\alpha \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ then $\alpha = 0$ or $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
</TheoremBox>

## Common Mistakes

> ⚠ **The zero vector is not the number 0.** In $\mathbb{P}_n$, the zero vector is the zero polynomial. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, it's $(0,0,0)$. In $\mathbb{R}^+$ with the exotic operations above, it's $1$.

> ⚠ **Axiom 10 says $1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$, not $0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$.** The number $0$ kills any vector (Theorem 4.i).

> ⚠ **Not every set with two operations is a vector space.** The ten axioms must all be checked — especially closure of scalar multiplication in the given field.

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Vector Spaces">
- A **vector space** over $\mathbb{F}$ is any set $V$ where addition (abelian group axioms) and scalar multiplication (4 axioms) are compatible via distributivity.
- Canonical examples: $\mathbb{F}^n$, $\mathbb{P}_n(\mathbb{F})$, $M_{m \times n}(\mathbb{F})$, solution spaces of homogeneous ODEs.
- The zero vector $\mathbf{0}$ is unique and $0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, $\alpha \cdot \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$ for all $\mathbf{v}, \alpha$.
- Exotic vector spaces exist — "vectors" need not be arrows.
- The scalar field matters: $\mathbb{C}^n$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ (dimension $n$) but also over $\mathbb{R}$ (dimension $2n$).
</SummaryBox>
