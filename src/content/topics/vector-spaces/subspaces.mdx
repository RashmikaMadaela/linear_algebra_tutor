## Big Idea

A **subspace** is a subset of a vector space that is itself a vector space under the *same* operations. Instead of checking all ten axioms again, there's a beautiful shortcut: just check closure under addition and scalar multiplication.

## Intuition

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, a line through the origin and a plane through the origin are both subspaces. A line *not* through the origin is not a subspace — it fails to contain $\mathbf{0}$.

The "through the origin" requirement is the key geometric insight: if you can add two vectors in a subspace, you must stay inside it; if you can scale any vector in it, you must stay inside it.

---

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={5} title="Subspace">
Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$. A non-empty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a **subspace** of $V$ if $W$ is itself a vector space over $\mathbb{F}$ under the same addition and scalar multiplication operations inherited from $V$.
</DefinitionBox>

---

## The Subspace Test

Checking all ten axioms would be tedious. The next theorem reduces it to just two checks — plus non-emptiness.

<TheoremBox number={5} title="Subspace Criterion (Closure Test)" proofSketch={<>The closure conditions (i) and (ii) ensure all the algebraic axioms hold in $W$. Since $W \subseteq V$, commutativity, associativity, and distributivity are inherited automatically from $V$. The zero vector follows from (ii) with $\alpha = 0$: $0 \cdot w = \mathbf{0} \in W$. Additive inverses follow from (ii) with $\alpha = -1$: $(-1)w = -w \in W$.</>}>
A non-empty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a subspace of $V$ if and only if:

1. **Closed under addition**: $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W \implies \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in W$
2. **Closed under scalar multiplication**: $\alpha \in \mathbb{F},\, \mathbf{v} \in W \implies \alpha \mathbf{v} \in W$

Equivalently: $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W$ and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{F}$ $\implies$ $\alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v} \in W$.
</TheoremBox>

> **Note:** The single combined condition $\alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{v} \in W$ (for all $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{F}$ and $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W$) is called **closure under linear combinations**, and is the most efficient way to check in practice.

## Intersection and Union

<TheoremBox number={5} title="Intersection of Subspaces is a Subspace" proofSketch={<>If $\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in W_1 \cap W_2$, then both are in $W_1$ (a subspace) so $\alpha\mathbf{v}+\beta\mathbf{w} \in W_1$, and similarly $\in W_2$. Hence $\alpha\mathbf{v}+\beta\mathbf{w} \in W_1 \cap W_2$.</>}>
If $W_1$ and $W_2$ are subspaces of $V$, then $W_1 \cap W_2$ is also a subspace of $V$.

> **Warning:** $W_1 \cup W_2$ is generally **not** a subspace. Take $W_1 = x$-axis and $W_2 = y$-axis in $\mathbb{R}^2$: $(1,0) \in W_1$ and $(0,1) \in W_2$, but $(1,0)+(0,1) = (1,1) \notin W_1 \cup W_2$.
</TheoremBox>

<SummaryBox title="Key Takeaways: Subspaces">
- A **subspace** $W \subseteq V$ must satisfy: $\mathbf{0} \in W$, closed under $+$, closed under scalar $\cdot$.
- The shortcut: just check $\alpha\mathbf{u}+\beta\mathbf{v} \in W$ for all $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{F}$, $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W$.
- Key examples: solution spaces of homogeneous linear ODEs, null spaces of matrices, $\mathbb{P}_k \subseteq \mathbb{P}_n$, planes through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
- **Intersection** of subspaces is a subspace; **union** is generally not.
- A non-homogeneous set (e.g., $ax+by+cz=d$ with $d\neq 0$) is never a subspace.
</SummaryBox>
