## Big Idea

Not every matrix is diagonalizable — when geometric multiplicity falls short of algebraic multiplicity, the best we can do is a "nearly diagonal" form.
**Jordan Normal Form** is that best form: block-diagonal, with eigenvalues on the diagonal and (possibly) ones on the superdiagonal.
It is the definitive answer to "what does a matrix really look like, up to similarity?"

## Intuition

A diagonal matrix $D$ acts on each basis vector by independent scaling: $D\mathbf{e}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{e}_i$.
A Jordan block $J_k(\lambda)$ acts almost like scaling, but with a "coupling" between adjacent basis vectors:

<BM math="J_k(\lambda)\mathbf{e}_1 = \lambda\mathbf{e}_1, \quad J_k(\lambda)\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{e}_1 + \lambda\mathbf{e}_2, \quad \ldots" />

The $1$s above the diagonal encode the **defect** — how much the eigenvectors are "entangled." When a Jordan block has size $k > 1$, the transformation has only one independent eigenvector for that eigenvalue, but $k$ "generalized eigenvectors."

Over $\mathbb{C}$, **every** matrix has a Jordan form. Over $\mathbb{R}$, complex eigenvalues appear in conjugate pairs and real Jordan form uses $2\times 2$ rotation-scaling blocks.

## Formal Definitions

<DefinitionBox number={32} title="Jordan Block and Jordan Normal Form">
A **Jordan block** of size $k$ with eigenvalue $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is the $k \times k$ matrix:

<BM math="J_k(\lambda) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 & & \\ & \lambda & 1 & \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & \lambda \end{pmatrix}" />

(eigenvalue $\lambda$ on the diagonal, $1$s on the superdiagonal, zeros elsewhere).

A matrix $J$ is in **Jordan Normal Form (JNF)** if it is block-diagonal with Jordan blocks:

<BM math="J = \operatorname{diag}(J_{k_1}(\lambda_1),\, J_{k_2}(\lambda_2),\, \ldots,\, J_{k_r}(\lambda_r))" />

The blocks need not be in any particular order, and the same $\lambda$ may appear in multiple blocks.

**The Jordan Decomposition** of $A$ is: $A = P J P^{-1}$ where $J$ is in Jordan Normal Form.
</DefinitionBox>

## Jordan Decomposition Theorem

<TheoremBox number={37} title="Existence of Jordan Normal Form" proofSketch={<>Over <M math="\mathbb{C}" />, the characteristic polynomial splits completely into linear factors. The proof constructs generalized eigenspaces <M math="\ker(A-\lambda I)^k" /> and shows they fill all of <M math="\mathbb{C}^n" />. Each generalized eigenspace has a Jordan chain basis. The construction is inductive and nonconstructive in general — the key fact is that <M math="\mathbb{C}" /> is algebraically closed.</>}>

Every matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ is similar to a matrix in Jordan Normal Form. That is, there exists an invertible $P \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ such that

<BM math="A = P J P^{-1}" />

where $J$ is in Jordan Normal Form. The JNF is **unique up to reordering of blocks**.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox number={38} title="Multiplicities and Jordan Structure" proofSketch={<>Count dimensions: the generalized eigenspace for <M math="\lambda" /> has dimension <M math="a_\lambda" /> (algebraic mult.). The number of Jordan blocks for <M math="\lambda" /> equals <M math="\dim\ker(A-\lambda I) = g_\lambda" /> (geometric mult.). The minimal polynomial exponent for <M math="\lambda" /> is the size of the largest Jordan block for <M math="\lambda" />.</>}>

Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ with distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_s$. For eigenvalue $\lambda_i$, let $J_{\lambda_i}$ be the collection of Jordan blocks for $\lambda_i$ with sizes $k_1 \geq k_2 \geq \cdots \geq k_{r_i}$. Then:

1. **Geometric multiplicity** $g_{\lambda_i} = r_i$ = number of Jordan blocks for $\lambda_i$
2. **Algebraic multiplicity** $a_{\lambda_i} = k_1 + k_2 + \cdots + k_{r_i}$ = sum of block sizes
3. **Minimal multiplicity** $m_{\lambda_i} = k_1$ = size of the **largest** Jordan block for $\lambda_i$

**Consequence (for $n \leq 3$):** $a_\lambda - g_\lambda = m_\lambda - 1$ (this holds when there is at most one Jordan block of maximum size, which is the generic case for small $n$).

**Diagonalizable $\Leftrightarrow$** all Jordan blocks have size 1 $\Leftrightarrow$ $g_\lambda = a_\lambda$ for all $\lambda$.
</TheoremBox>

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Jordan Normal Form — Key Facts">

**Structure:** $J = \bigoplus_i J_{k_i}(\lambda_i)$ — block diagonal, one Jordan block per generalized eigenspace chain.

**Reading off multiplicities from JNF:**
- $g_\lambda$ = number of Jordan blocks with eigenvalue $\lambda$
- $a_\lambda$ = total size of all blocks with eigenvalue $\lambda$  
- $m_\lambda$ = size of the largest block with eigenvalue $\lambda$ (= exponent of $(\lambda-\lambda_0)$ in minimal polynomial)

**Special cases:**
- All $k_i = 1$: $J = D$ diagonal, $A$ is diagonalizable
- Single $k_1 = n$ (one block): $A$ is a single Jordan block (maximally defective)

**Powers and functions of $J_k(\lambda)$:**

<BM math="J_k(\lambda)^m = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda^m & \binom{m}{1}\lambda^{m-1} & \binom{m}{2}\lambda^{m-2} & \cdots \\ 0 & \lambda^m & \binom{m}{1}\lambda^{m-1} & \cdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & & & \lambda^m\end{pmatrix}" />
</SummaryBox>
