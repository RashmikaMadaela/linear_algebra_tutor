## Big Idea

Every matrix $A$ with linearly independent columns can be factored as $A = QR$ where $Q$ has orthonormal columns and $R$ is upper triangular.
This is essentially **Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization** encoded as a matrix factorization.
The deeper application is the **QR algorithm** — an iterative procedure that converges to the eigenvalues of $A$ without ever explicitly solving the characteristic polynomial.

## Intuition

Think of $A$'s columns as a set of linearly independent vectors.
Gram-Schmidt turns them into an orthonormal set — that's $Q$.
The matrix $R$ records how you can write the original columns back in terms of $Q$: it stores the coefficients of the Gram-Schmidt process.

The QR algorithm exploits a beautiful fact: repeatedly replacing $A_k$ with $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k$ (swap the factors after each QR decomposition) drives the matrix toward upper triangular form, with eigenvalues appearing on the diagonal.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={33} title="QR Decomposition">
Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$ ($m \geq n$) have linearly independent columns.
The **QR decomposition** of $A$ is

<BM math="A = QR" />

where:
- $Q \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$ has **orthonormal columns**: $Q^H Q = I_n$
- $R \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ is **upper triangular** with positive real diagonal entries

The decomposition is **unique** when $A$ has full column rank and $R$ has positive diagonal.

**Square case:** If $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ is invertible, then $Q$ is unitary ($QQ^H = I_n$) and $R$ is invertible.
</DefinitionBox>

## QR via Gram-Schmidt

<TheoremBox number={39} title="QR Decomposition via Gram-Schmidt" proofSketch={<>Apply Gram-Schmidt to the columns <M math="\mathbf{a}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{a}_n" /> of <M math="A" />, producing orthonormal vectors <M math="\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n" />. Each original column is a linear combination of earlier <M math="\mathbf{q}_i" />'s: <M math="\mathbf{a}_j = \sum_{i\leq j} r_{ij}\mathbf{q}_i" /> with <M math="r_{ij} = \langle\mathbf{a}_j,\mathbf{q}_i\rangle" />. Assembling into matrix form gives <M math="A = QR" />.</>}>

Every $A \in \mathbb{C}^{m\times n}$ with linearly independent columns has a QR decomposition $A = QR$.

**Construction via Gram-Schmidt:** Let $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n$ be the columns of $A$. Define:

<BM math="\mathbf{u}_j = \mathbf{a}_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1} \langle \mathbf{a}_j, \mathbf{q}_i \rangle\, \mathbf{q}_i, \qquad \mathbf{q}_j = \frac{\mathbf{u}_j}{\|\mathbf{u}_j\|}" />

Then $Q = [\mathbf{q}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{q}_n]$ and

<BM math="R = \begin{pmatrix} \|\mathbf{u}_1\| & \langle\mathbf{a}_2,\mathbf{q}_1\rangle & \langle\mathbf{a}_3,\mathbf{q}_1\rangle & \cdots \\ 0 & \|\mathbf{u}_2\| & \langle\mathbf{a}_3,\mathbf{q}_2\rangle & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & \|\mathbf{u}_3\| & \cdots \\ \vdots & & & \ddots \end{pmatrix}" />

The diagonal entries $r_{jj} = \|\mathbf{u}_j\| > 0$ are positive.
</TheoremBox>

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="QR Decomposition — Key Facts">

**QR existence:** Any $A$ with full column rank has $A = QR$ (unique with positive diagonal of $R$).

**Applications:**
- **Least squares:** Solve $A\mathbf{x} \approx \mathbf{b}$ via $R\mathbf{x} = Q^T\mathbf{b}$ (numerically stable)
- **Eigenvalues:** QR algorithm drives $A$ to upper triangular
- **Orthonormal basis:** Columns of $Q$ form orthonormal basis for $\operatorname{col}(A)$

**QR algorithm:** $A_0 = A$; iterate $A_k = Q_kR_k$, $A_{k+1} = R_kQ_k$. All iterates are similar to $A$; converges to Schur form with eigenvalues on diagonal.
</SummaryBox>
