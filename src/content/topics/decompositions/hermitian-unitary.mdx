## Big Idea

Over the reals, symmetric matrices ($A^T = A$) are the "nicest" matrices — they have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.
Over the complex numbers, the right analog is the **Hermitian matrix** ($A^H = A$).
**Unitary matrices** are the complex generalization of orthogonal matrices.
Together they give the strongest possible diagonalization result: the **Spectral Theorem**.

## Intuition

The transpose $A^T$ reflects a matrix across its main diagonal. The **conjugate transpose** (Hermitian adjoint) $A^H = \overline{A}^T$ does the same thing but also takes the complex conjugate of each entry.

A Hermitian matrix is "symmetric in the complex sense": the entry in row $i$, column $j$ is the complex conjugate of the entry in row $j$, column $i$.
This symmetry forces all eigenvalues to be real — you can't rotate directions when the matrix equals its own adjoint.

A unitary matrix $P$ preserves lengths and angles in $\mathbb{C}^n$: $\|P\mathbf{x}\| = \|\mathbf{x}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}$.
Its columns form an **orthonormal basis** for $\mathbb{C}^n$.

## Formal Definitions

<DefinitionBox number={29} title="Hermitian Matrix">
Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$. The **conjugate transpose** (Hermitian adjoint) of $A$ is

<BM math="A^H = \overline{A}^T \quad \text{i.e.,} \quad (A^H)_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}" />

$A$ is **Hermitian** (or **self-adjoint**) if $A^H = A$, i.e., $A_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}$ for all $i, j$.

**Real case:** A real Hermitian matrix is simply a **symmetric** matrix ($A^T = A$).

**Consequence:** Diagonal entries of a Hermitian matrix satisfy $a_{ii} = \overline{a_{ii}}$, so they are all **real**.
</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox number={30} title="Unitary Matrix">
$P \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ is **unitary** if

<BM math="P^H P = P P^H = I_n" />

Equivalently: $P^{-1} = P^H$.

**Real case:** A real unitary matrix is an **orthogonal** matrix ($P^T P = I$, i.e., $P^{-1} = P^T$).

**Characterization:** $P$ is unitary $\iff$ the columns of $P$ form an orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{C}^n$ with the standard Hermitian inner product $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^H \mathbf{v}$.
</DefinitionBox>

## Spectral Theorem

<TheoremBox number={35} title="Spectral Theorem for Hermitian Matrices" proofSketch={<>Every Hermitian matrix is unitarily diagonalizable: <M math="A = PDP^H" /> where <M math="P" /> is unitary and <M math="D" /> is real diagonal. The proof uses induction on <M math="n" />: find one eigenvalue/eigenvector, write the complementary subspace, and show the restriction of <M math="A" /> to that subspace is also Hermitian.</>}>

Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ be Hermitian ($A^H = A$). Then:

1. **All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.**
2. **Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal** (with respect to $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^H \mathbf{v}$).
3. **$A$ is unitarily diagonalizable:** there exists unitary $P$ and real diagonal $D$ such that

<BM math="A = P D P^H" />

4. **Real symmetric case:** if $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with $A^T = A$, then $P$ can be chosen to be a real **orthogonal** matrix.

**Proof of (1):** Let $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ with $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Compute:

<BM math="\lambda \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle A\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{v}^H A \mathbf{v}" />

Since $A = A^H$:

<BM math="\overline{\lambda} \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \overline{\langle A\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle} = \langle \mathbf{v}, A\mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{v}^H A^H \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}^H A \mathbf{v}" />

Therefore $\lambda \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \overline{\lambda} \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$, and since $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 > 0$ we get $\lambda = \overline{\lambda}$, so $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$. $\square$

**Proof of (2):** Let $A\mathbf{u} = \lambda\mathbf{u}$ and $A\mathbf{v} = \mu\mathbf{v}$ with $\lambda \neq \mu$ (both real). Then:

<BM math="\lambda \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle A\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{v}^H A \mathbf{u} = (A^H \mathbf{v})^H \mathbf{u} = (A\mathbf{v})^H \mathbf{u} = \mu \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle" />

So $(\lambda - \mu)\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$. Since $\lambda \neq \mu$, we get $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$. $\square$
</TheoremBox>

## Key Properties

<SummaryBox title="Hermitian & Unitary — Key Facts">

| Property | Hermitian $A = A^H$ | Unitary $P^H P = I$ |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Eigenvalues | All **real** | All on unit circle $|\lambda|=1$ |
| Eigenvectors | **Orthogonal** for distinct $\lambda$ | — |
| Diagonalization | $A = PDP^H$, $P$ unitary, $D$ real diagonal | Columns form orthonormal basis |
| Real case | Symmetric $A^T = A$ | Orthogonal $P^T P = I$ |
| Determinant | Real | $|\det P| = 1$ |
| Inverse | $A^{-1}$ is also Hermitian (if exists) | $P^{-1} = P^H$ |

**Mnemonic:** Hermitian is to complex matrices what symmetric is to real matrices. Unitary is to complex matrices what orthogonal is to real matrices.
</SummaryBox>
