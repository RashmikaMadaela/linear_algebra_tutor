## Big Idea

A real number is "positive" if it sits to the right of zero on the number line.
For matrices, **positive definiteness** is the matrix analog: a Hermitian matrix $A$ is positive definite if the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^H A \mathbf{x}$ is strictly positive for every nonzero vector $\mathbf{x}$.
This concept appears everywhere — in optimization (Hessians of convex functions), in statistics (covariance matrices), and in physics (energy functionals).

## Intuition

The quadratic form $q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ assigns a scalar to every vector.
For $A = I$, $q(\mathbf{x}) = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 > 0$ — the identity is positive definite.
For $A = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$, $q(0,1) = -1 < 0$ — this is **indefinite**.

Geometrically, $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = c$ traces an **ellipsoid** when $A$ is positive definite and a **hyperboloid** (saddle) when $A$ is indefinite. The eigenvalues control the shape: all positive = ellipsoid, some negative = saddle.

## Formal Definition

<DefinitionBox number={31} title="Positive Definite Matrix">
A Hermitian matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ (so $A^H = A$) is:

- **Positive definite (PD):** $\mathbf{x}^H A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Written $A \succ 0$.
- **Positive semidefinite (PSD):** $\mathbf{x}^H A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}$. Written $A \succeq 0$.
- **Negative definite (ND):** $\mathbf{x}^H A \mathbf{x} < 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Written $A \prec 0$.
- **Indefinite:** neither PD nor ND — quadratic form takes both positive and negative values.

**Real case:** For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ symmetric, PD means $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.
</DefinitionBox>

## Characterization Theorem

<TheoremBox number={36} title="Equivalent Conditions for Positive Definiteness" proofSketch={<>The eigenvalue criterion follows from the Spectral Theorem: writing <M math="\mathbf{x} = P\mathbf{y}" /> where <M math="P" /> is the unitary diagonalizer of <M math="A" />, we get <M math="\mathbf{x}^H A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^H D\mathbf{y} = \sum_i \lambda_i |y_i|^2" />. This is positive for all <M math="\mathbf{y}\neq\mathbf{0}" /> iff all <M math="\lambda_i > 0" />.</>}>

For a Hermitian matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$, the following are equivalent:

1. $A$ is **positive definite** ($A \succ 0$)
2. All **eigenvalues** of $A$ are positive ($\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$)
3. All **leading principal minors** are positive:

<BM math="\det(A_{1:k, 1:k}) > 0 \quad \text{for } k = 1, 2, \ldots, n" />

4. $A = B^H B$ for some invertible matrix $B$ (**Cholesky-like factorization**)
5. There exists an invertible lower-triangular $L$ with positive diagonal such that $A = LL^H$ (**Cholesky decomposition**)

**Proof of (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (2):** By the Spectral Theorem (Thm 35), $A = PDP^H$ with $P$ unitary. For any $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$, set $\mathbf{y} = P^H\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$ (since $P^H$ is invertible). Then:

<BM math="\mathbf{x}^H A \mathbf{x} = (P\mathbf{y})^H A (P\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{y}^H P^H A P \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{y}^H D \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i |y_i|^2" />

This is $> 0$ for all $\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{0}$ iff all $\lambda_i > 0$. $\square$
</TheoremBox>

## Summary

<SummaryBox title="Positive Definite — Key Facts">

**Test for PD:** check all eigenvalues > 0, OR all leading principal minors > 0.

**Consequences of $A \succ 0$:**
- $\det(A) > 0$, all diagonal entries $> 0$
- $A$ is invertible; $A^{-1}$ is also PD
- Unique Cholesky decomposition $A = LL^H$ exists
- The quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^H A\mathbf{x} = c$ defines an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (for real $A$)

**Key examples:**
- $I_n \succ 0$
- $A^H A \succeq 0$ always; $A^H A \succ 0 \iff A$ has full column rank
- Diagonal matrix $D = \operatorname{diag}(d_1,\ldots,d_n) \succ 0 \iff$ all $d_i > 0$
</SummaryBox>
